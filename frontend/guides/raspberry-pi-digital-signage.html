Raspberry Pi for Digital Signage: Is It Worth It for Your Business?

By ScreenFizz June 2026 5 min read

A Raspberry Pi can absolutely run digital signage. But for most independent business owners, there are simpler and more reliable ways to get a screen up and running. Here is an honest assessment of when a Pi makes sense, and when it does not.

What is a Raspberry Pi and why do people use it for signage?

A Raspberry Pi is a small, affordable single-board computer (typically £35–75 depending on the model) that runs Linux and can drive a screen via HDMI. Because it is cheap and flexible, it became popular among hobbyists and technically minded businesses as a DIY digital signage player.

Pi-based signage can work very well in the right hands. The Pi 4 and Pi 5 are capable of playing full 1080p video smoothly, and with the right software they can be locked down to run reliably as a dedicated display.

The case for using a Pi

  • Low hardware cost. A Pi 4 with a case and power supply comes to around £60–80, comparable to a dedicated signage player.
  • Flexibility. If you or someone in your team is comfortable with Linux, you have full control over the setup.
  • Good for technical environments. IT teams in schools, offices, and corporate settings often use Pi-based signage to keep infrastructure in-house.

The case against using a Pi for your business

For most independent businesses, cafes, restaurants, barbers, retailers, a Pi-based setup creates more problems than it solves:

  • Setup requires technical knowledge. Getting a Pi to run reliably as a signage player involves flashing an OS, running scripts, configuring kiosk mode, and preventing the screen from sleeping. This is not difficult for a developer, but it is not plug-and-play.
  • Ongoing maintenance falls on you. SD cards fail. Software updates break things. The Pi needs someone to fix it when it stops working, and "my screen is frozen" at 8am on a Monday is not what you want.
  • Content updates require effort. Updating what is on your screen typically requires logging into a platform, editing content, and publishing. That is time you are spending away from your customers.
  • No support. If your DIY Pi setup breaks, there is no one to call.

Who should use a Pi for signage?

  • IT professionals or developers who enjoy the technical challenge
  • Schools, universities, or corporate environments with in-house technical staff
  • Hobbyists building custom display projects
  • Businesses that need offline or air-gapped signage on a locked-down network

If you run a small business and just want a professional screen that works, a managed service will serve you far better than a Pi project.
