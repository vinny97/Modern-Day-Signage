Affordable Advertising Menu Boards for Small Businesses: What to Buy and What to Avoid

By ScreenFizz 26 June 2026 7 min read

If you run a cafe, takeaway, salon, shop, gym or local venue, an advertising menu board can be one of the simplest ways to make your business look sharper and sell more. The problem is that the market can feel confusing very quickly.

You can buy a cheap TV. You can buy digital signage software. You can pay a designer. You can try to build your own menu in Canva. You can upload files yourself. You can also spend weeks comparing systems when all you really wanted was a screen that shows your offers properly.

This guide explains what a small business actually needs, where cheap menu boards go wrong, and why a fully managed service is often the better affordable option.

First, what is an advertising menu board?

An advertising menu board is a screen that shows prices, offers, services, meal deals, product highlights or customer information. It might sit above a counter, in a window, behind reception or in a waiting area.

For food businesses, it might show menus and specials. For barbers and salons, it might show services and prices. For gyms, it might show memberships and class times. For shops, it might show promotions, new arrivals and seasonal offers.

The aim is simple: customers should understand what you sell, what is worth buying today, and what to do next.

What makes a menu board affordable?

The cheapest option is not always the most affordable option. A cheap screen that never gets updated is not really saving you money. It becomes wallpaper. Customers stop noticing it, staff forget about it, and your offers stay out of date.

A genuinely affordable menu board has three things:

  • Low setup cost so you can get started without a big project.
  • Low monthly cost so it makes sense for a small business budget.
  • Low effort so it actually gets used every week.

That third point is the one many people miss. If updating your board takes a laptop, a login, a template, a file export and ten spare minutes you do not have, it will not stay fresh.

The practical test: if your prices changed tomorrow, how quickly could your screen be updated without you doing the technical work?

The cheapest route: a TV and a USB stick

The lowest-cost way to create a menu board is to put an image or video on a USB stick and plug it into a TV. This can work for a short-term event or a one-off promotion.

For a real business, it usually falls apart. Someone has to design the graphic, export the file, find the USB stick, plug it in, check it works, and repeat the process every time something changes.

It also gives you no easy scheduling, no remote updates, no monitoring and no simple way to fix the screen if something stops playing. It is cheap at the start, but expensive in time.

The software route: low price, more responsibility

Digital signage software can be useful if you have someone in the business who wants to manage screens. You pay a monthly fee, log in, upload content, build playlists and schedule what appears.

That sounds simple until you are running a lunch rush, taking bookings, managing staff and trying to remember whether the Friday offer is still showing on the screen.

Software is not the wrong answer for every business. But if you are a small local business owner who wants the result without learning another platform, software alone is not the easiest answer.

For many small businesses, this is the sweet spot. You get a professional result without hiring a designer, buying complicated software or giving staff another admin job.

What should a small business menu board show?

A good advertising menu board does not need to show everything. In fact, showing too much is one of the easiest ways to make a screen hard to read.

Start with the message that helps customers decide faster or spend more confidently:

  • Your most popular menu items or services
  • Today's special or current offer
  • Best-value bundles or meal deals
  • Opening hours and ordering instructions
  • Seasonal promotions
  • High-margin items you want more people to notice

The board should be simple enough to understand in a few seconds. If someone has to stand still and study it, the design is doing too much.

What to avoid when buying a cheap advertising menu board

Before spending money, look out for these common traps:

  • A screen with no update plan. If nobody owns the updates, the board will go stale.
  • Designs that are too busy. Tiny text and crowded menus are hard to read from a queue.
  • Software nobody has time to use. A low monthly price is not helpful if it creates more admin.
  • No support when something goes wrong. Screens are customer-facing, so downtime matters.
  • One-off graphics that never change. Digital signage works best when it stays current.

How to decide what you need

Ask yourself three questions:

  1. Do I already have a suitable TV or screen?
  2. Do I want to manage the content myself?
  3. How often will prices, offers or menus change?

If you already have a TV and only need a simple display, your setup can be very low cost. If you need a screen, player, design and ongoing updates, it is still possible to keep the project affordable, but it should be planned properly.

The main thing is to avoid buying pieces in the wrong order. A screen is only useful when the content, setup and update process are sorted too.
