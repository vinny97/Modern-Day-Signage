Digital Signage ROI for Small Businesses: What to Measure

By ScreenFizz6 July 20266 min read

A business screen should earn its place. It should help the business make money, save time or improve the customer experience in a visible way.

That is where digital signage ROI matters. Small businesses do not need complicated dashboards to work out whether a screen is worth it. They need a simple way to connect the screen to better outcomes.

This guide explains which metrics matter and how to measure them without turning the exercise into a full-time job.

What digital signage ROI means

Return on investment compares the value created by a screen with the full cost of running it. For sales-led campaigns, use this calculation:

ROI = (additional gross profit minus signage cost) divided by signage cost, multiplied by 100.

Use gross profit rather than revenue where possible. A £10 sale is not worth £10 after stock, ingredients or fulfilment costs.

Start with one clear job

Define what the screen should achieve before measuring it. A window display might bring more people inside, while a menu board can guide customers towards a profitable bundle.

  • Increase average transaction value with add-ons or upgrades.
  • Sell more of one product with a focused promotion.
  • Generate enquiries or bookings for a named service.
  • Save staff time by answering repeated questions.

Choose one primary outcome for each campaign. Trying to prove every possible benefit at once creates vague data.

Record a fair baseline

Measure normal performance for two to four weeks before changing the screen. Record the metric that matches the goal: units sold, average order value, enquiry count, booking count or minutes of staff time.

Compare like with like. Saturdays should be compared with Saturdays, and seasonal periods need a comparable reference. Note price changes, stock shortages, local events and unusual weather that could distort the result.

Five practical tracking methods

1. Compare promoted product sales

Run one offer and compare units sold with the baseline. Keep its price, availability and staff script stable so the screen is the main change.

2. Use a screen-only code

Display a short offer code or ask customers to mention a named promotion. Redemptions provide a clear attribution signal, although they will not capture every influenced sale.

3. Track a dedicated QR link

Send the QR code to a unique page or tagged booking link. Measure completed forms, purchases or bookings—not scans alone. A scan shows attention, not return.

4. Add a till or booking-system button

A “screen promotion” button lets staff record influenced sales and enquiries. Keep the process quick and define exactly when the button should be used.

5. Estimate time saved

If the screen answers common questions, record how often staff used to answer them and how long each answer took. Multiply the hours saved by a realistic hourly employment cost.

Run a simple four-week test

  1. Choose one product or customer action with enough volume to measure.
  2. Record a two-to-four-week baseline.
  3. Run one clear message for four weeks.
  4. Track the result weekly using sales data, codes, conversions or staff records.
  5. Compare additional gross profit or time saved with the campaign's share of costs.

If possible, alternate the campaign on and off across comparable days. Repeated improvement when the message is visible is stronger evidence than one before-and-after comparison.

Metrics that can mislead

  • Total sales: too many factors affect overall revenue; track the promoted item instead.
  • QR scans: useful for interest, but completed actions matter more.
  • Estimated impressions: views do not prove behaviour changed.
  • Customer compliments: positive feedback helps, but is not a commercial measure.

Include the full cost

Count the display, player, installation, software, content design, maintenance and staff time. Spread equipment costs across the expected working life rather than charging them all to one short campaign.

A managed digital signage service can simplify this calculation because the ongoing cost is predictable and content stays current.

Improve weak results one change at a time

A weak first test does not automatically mean the screen has no value. Check whether customers can read the message, understand the benefit and act before the slide changes.

Change one variable—the headline, offer, image, call to action, position or timing—then test again. A focused digital menu board experiment produces clearer learning than replacing every slide at once.

The bottom line

Give the screen one commercial job, establish a fair baseline and track the customer action closest to revenue or cost savings. The result will not capture every influenced purchase, but it will show what works and whether the screen is earning its place.
