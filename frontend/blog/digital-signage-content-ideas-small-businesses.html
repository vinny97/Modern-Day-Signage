30 Digital Signage Content Ideas for Small Businesses

By ScreenFizz 25 June 2026 6 min read

The hardest part of digital signage is rarely the screen. It is knowing what to put on it after the first week.

A good business screen should do more than fill space. It should answer questions, sell the right products, reduce pressure on staff, and make the place feel more polished. The best content is simple, useful, and changed often enough that regular customers keep noticing it.

Here are 30 digital signage content ideas any small business can use, whether you run a shop, cafe, salon, gym, clinic, showroom or local venue.

Sales and promotion ideas

  1. Today's featured productPick one item you want customers to notice first and give it the hero treatment.
  2. Limited-time offersUse your screen for offers that only run this week, this weekend, or during quieter hours.
  3. Bundle dealsShow simple combinations like coffee and cake, haircut and beard trim, or starter and main.
  4. New arrivalsLet regular customers know what has just landed without relying on staff to mention it every time.
  5. Seasonal productsPromote summer drinks, Christmas gifting, back-to-school offers, or payday treats when they matter most.
  6. Upsell promptsSuggest the next natural add-on, such as extra syrup, premium toppings, aftercare products, or an extended appointment.

Helpful customer information

  1. Opening hoursShow current hours clearly, especially around bank holidays, events, and seasonal changes.
  2. Price listsKeep your prices clean, readable, and easy to update when services or costs change.
  3. How to order or bookExplain the next step: order at the counter, scan the QR code, book online, or speak to reception.
  4. Wait timesIf customers often ask how long something will take, put the answer where they can see it.
  5. Allergen and dietary notesShow clear labels for vegan, vegetarian, gluten-free, dairy-free, or nut-free options.
  6. FAQsAnswer the questions your team hears every day, from parking details to cancellation rules.

Trust-building content

  1. Customer reviewsDisplay short, real testimonials that make new visitors feel more confident.
  2. Before and after photosPerfect for salons, barbers, clinics, gyms, trades, and home improvement businesses.
  3. Team introductionsPut faces to names and make the business feel more personal.
  4. Behind-the-scenes clipsShow products being made, food being prepared, rooms being set up, or staff at work.
  5. Certifications and awardsIf you have accreditations, hygiene ratings, local awards or press mentions, your screen can reinforce them.
  6. Case studiesShow a quick customer story: the problem, the service, and the result.

Community and brand content

  1. Local eventsPromote events your customers care about, especially if they bring people into your area.
  2. Charity or fundraiser updatesShow progress, thank customers, and make participation visible.
  3. Social media promptsAsk customers to follow, tag, review, or share, but keep the instruction short.
  4. Customer photosWith permission, feature customers using your product, wearing your work, or attending your venue.
  5. Brand storyShare why you started, what you specialise in, or what makes your service different.
  6. MilestonesCelebrate anniversaries, new branches, new team members, or a big number of customers served.

Operational screen content

  1. Queue guidanceTell people where to stand, where to collect, or which desk to visit.
  2. Safety noticesUse calm, well-designed notices for wet floors, equipment rules, entry instructions, or hygiene reminders.
  3. Appointment remindersPrompt customers to rebook before they leave or check in when they arrive.
  4. Staff announcementsKeep internal teams aligned with shift notes, daily targets, or service updates.
  5. Live dashboardsShow booking slots, table availability, class times, or pickup status when useful.
  6. Emergency messagesHave a simple fallback message ready for closures, delays, outages, or urgent changes.

How to choose what goes on your screen

Start with the customer journey. What are people doing when they see the screen? Waiting, deciding, browsing, queueing, checking in, or walking past? The answer should guide the content.

A window display needs fast, bold messages that make people stop. A screen behind a counter can carry prices, bundles and menus. A waiting-room screen can tell a deeper story because people have more time to read.

The rule is simple: put the most useful message in the place where the customer is most ready to act on it.

Keep it fresh without making it complicated

You do not need 30 things on screen at once. In fact, you should avoid that. Pick three or four content types and rotate them well. A cafe might use menu panels, specials, reviews and Instagram prompts. A salon might use service pricing, before and after photos, staff introductions and rebooking reminders.

The real win is having a system that is easy to update. If changing a screen requires design software, cables, USB sticks or logging into awkward software, it will not happen often. If you can send a quick message and have someone update it for you, your screen becomes part of how the business actually runs.
